# Chapter 1 final manuscript strategy

## One-sentence claim

Visible within-species phenotypic variation and climate tracking are distinct dimensions of capitulum-trait lability across thistles.

## Problem

Large comparative trait studies usually collapse a species to one mean and treat “lability” as a single property. This obscures two different questions: how much visible phenotype varies within a species, and how consistently that phenotype tracks environmental gradients. Public photographs provide enough replicated observations to separate these axes, but image assessability, circular hue, spatial structure and uncertain phylogenetic placement must be handled explicitly.

## Gap

No existing workflow for thistle capitulum traits jointly:

1. extracts continuous orientation, colour and shape from public photographs;
2. quantifies assessability rather than treating unmeasurable images as biological absence;
3. separates among-species climatic structure from within-species association;
4. decomposes species lability into total within-species variation and environmental responsiveness;
5. propagates uncertain within-genus grafting into historical-signal sensitivity.

## Main results to lead with

1. The final lability cohort contains 102 taxa meeting the completeness rule.
2. Within-species variation and environmental responsiveness are moderately negatively associated (Spearman rho = -0.333), so high variation does not imply strong climate tracking.
3. Colour has the highest median climate responsiveness but the lowest median total variation; shape shows the reverse contrast.
4. Four pooled endpoint–climate coefficients pass BH FDR, but standardized effects are small and must not be described as adaptation.
5. Minimum-sample sensitivity preserves species rankings.
6. Apparent phylogenetic signal is not robust to direct-backbone restriction; circularity and solidity are grafting-sensitive, and hue requires a joint circular test.

## Narrative architecture

### Introduction

Paragraph 1: why within-species trait variation matters for ecology, evolution and forecasting.

Paragraph 2: why comparative datasets normally erase it, and why “lability” conflates dispersion with environmental tracking.

Paragraph 3: opportunity and danger of public-image phenomics—scale, repeated observations and multi-trait extraction, balanced against viewpoint, colour calibration, detectability and spatial bias.

Paragraph 4: thistle capitula as a modular system. Orientation, colour and outline may experience different ecological filters, so a single conserved–labile axis is unlikely to suffice.

Paragraph 5: questions and predictions. We ask whether variation and responsiveness are coupled, whether modules occupy different positions in this two-dimensional space, and whether patterns survive sampling and phylogenetic sensitivity tests.

### Results

1. Dataset and measurement assessability.
2. Global pooled climate associations, emphasizing small effect sizes.
3. Species-level two-axis lability and four quadrants.
4. Module contrast among orientation, colour and shape.
5. Sensitivity and historical-constraint results.

### Discussion

1. The central conceptual result: variation and responsiveness are not interchangeable.
2. Modular trait architecture: colour tracks measured climate more than its total variability suggests, whereas shape contains substantial variation not explained by the four CHELSA gradients.
3. Methodological contribution: public-image phenomics can recover replicated within-species structure when assessability and provenance are modeled explicitly.
4. Boundaries: observational association, imperfect colour calibration, image vertical as gravity proxy, viewpoint-dependent shape and unresolved reticulate/polyploid history.
5. Broader implication: trait databases should retain distributions and environmental slopes, not only species means.

## Claim boundary

Use “visible within-species variation”, “environmental responsiveness”, “climate tracking” and “historical sensitivity”. Do not use the data to claim plasticity, local adaptation, evolutionary rate, pollinator causation or a resolved Cirsium species tree.

## Submission strategy

The strongest positioning is methodological-conceptual macroecology rather than a single-trait natural-history paper. The title should foreground decoupling, public photographs and capitulum trait modules. The analysis should now be frozen except for checks that can change credibility—not scope—of the central claim: measurement demonstration, detector performance, taxonomic freeze, residual spatial diagnostics and durable archiving.

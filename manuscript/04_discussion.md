# Discussion

## Species means conceal the dominant visible scale of the image record

A species mean is an incomplete representation of visible capitulum phenotype. Across all nine endpoints, most variance in the balanced image atlas occurred among records assigned to the same species rather than among species means. This does not imply that 82–99% of capitulum variation is heritable, adaptive or wholly biological. Public photographs combine population and individual differences with developmental state, viewpoint, illumination and measurement error. The supported conclusion is narrower: a single species mean discards most of the visible structure present in the observation archive.

The multidimensional PCA strengthens this point. No single axis captured orientation, colour and outline, and three components were required to explain about 69% of species-level variation. Comparative datasets based only on means therefore lose both distributional and multivariate information.

## Precision-aware reanalysis removes the apparent negative lability axis

The most important revision concerns the relationship between visible variation and within-species environmental association. The original score averaged absolute, unpooled species slopes. That construction is statistically biased because imprecise slope estimates have large absolute values even when their true effects are zero. The near-perfect correlations of the legacy score with sample size and standard error showed that the previously reported negative relation and median-split quadrants were not biologically interpretable.

After equal module weighting and explicit subtraction or modelling of slope sampling variance, no common relationship remained. The transparent noise-adjusted score was independent of slope sample size and gave a near-zero correlation with visible variation. A hierarchical variance meta-regression using all 2,828 slope estimates and their standard errors reached the same conclusion. We therefore do not infer that highly variable species are systematically less environmentally associated. Instead, the revised result is the absence of a detectable common cross-module coupling.

This correction sharpens the conceptual contribution. Dispersion and spatial environment–trait association remain different quantities, but their distinction is not demonstrated by a negative species ranking. It is demonstrated by their different definitions, uncertainty structures and scale-specific evidence. Any future cross-species lability index should model precision rather than rank raw absolute slopes.

## Trait architecture remains modular

Removing the legacy lability axis does not erase module-specific descriptive patterns. Shape retains the greatest visible image variance, while orientation and visible colour show stronger among-species environmental separation than most outline traits. This combination is consistent with shape measurements being especially sensitive to viewpoint and segmentation, while orientation and colour retain some geographic organization. It is not evidence that one module is more adaptively labile than another.

Orientation changes the angle at which capitula encounter radiation, precipitation and visitors. Experiments in other plant systems establish plausible thermal and behavioural pathways, but the global observations cannot distinguish thermal regulation, rain exposure, visitor presentation or stem mechanics. Visible colour likewise reflects camera-recorded lightness, chroma and hue rather than pigment concentration, ultraviolet reflectance or receiver-specific colour space. Mechanistic interpretation requires standardized photography, spectroscopy, pigment chemistry and experiments.

## Environmental structure is scale and cohort dependent

The exhaustive 46,276-observation cohort produced four small BH-supported non-circular linear associations, whereas the earlier balanced-atlas ≤10 km sensitivity produced none. This is not a strict-versus-expanded contradiction; it is a difference between a large post-detection spatially thinned cohort and a smaller balanced atlas. The revised cohort ledger makes this distinction explicit.

Within-species coefficients ask whether observations carrying the same species name shift along spatial climate gradients after species means are removed. Among-species environmental sorting asks whether species with different mean phenotypes occupy different environments. Neither implies temporal response, reaction norms or local adaptation. We therefore replace *climate tracking* and *environmental responsiveness* with *within-species spatial environment–trait association*.

## Public-image phenomics is valuable only when observation error remains visible

Public images provide geographic and within-species replication unavailable in most trait databases, but they trade standardization for scale. Detector false negatives, camera colour processing, image roll, occlusion and broad species concepts can all structure apparent variation. The real-image YOLO and measurement figure demonstrates the production chain, not measurement accuracy. Independent detector and continuous-trait validation remain necessary before final submission.

The revised analysis illustrates a broader methodological lesson: repeated public observations do not automatically solve uncertainty. Large archives can produce precise-looking species rankings that are dominated by unequal sample size unless estimation error is propagated. Distributional summaries, cohort ledgers, trait-specific assessability and precision-aware hierarchical models should be treated as core components of image-based macroecology.

## Historical and taxonomic limits

Only 54 of 216 image-analysis taxa were directly represented in the dated backbone, and no non-circular endpoint retained direct-tip FDR support. Tree placement therefore remains a sensitivity analysis rather than a resolved explanation. An authority-backed taxonomic freeze is equally important because lumped taxa, synonyms and uncertain identifications can alter both within- and among-species variance.

## Remaining tests

Before journal submission, the independent detector audit, continuous-measurement validation, accepted-name table and residual spatial and leave-one-region-out diagnostics must be completed. These tests may change the magnitude of visible variance or individual environmental coefficients. The principal revised claim is deliberately conditional: current data show large within-assigned-species image dispersion, multidimensional trait structure and module-dependent environmental sorting, but no common coupling between visible variation and within-species climate-association magnitude after slope uncertainty is modelled.

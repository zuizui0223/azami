# Discussion

### Species means conceal the dominant visible scale of variation

Our results show that a species mean is an incomplete representation of visible capitulum phenotype. Across all nine endpoints, most estimated variance occurred among observations assigned to the same species rather than among species means. This pattern does not imply that 82-99% of capitulum variation is heritable, adaptive or even wholly biological. Public photographs mix population differences, individual and ontogenetic variation, viewing geometry, illumination and measurement error. The important result is narrower but still consequential: once repeated observations are retained, the visible phenotype assigned to a species is broad enough that a single mean discards most of the observed structure.

This conclusion extends, rather than contradicts, previous work on intraspecific trait variation. Field-based studies and meta-analyses have shown that the contribution of within-species variation depends strongly on the trait and ecological scale (Messier et al. 2010; Albert et al. 2011; Siefert et al. 2015). Our fractions should not be compared directly with standardized field measurements because the observation process differs. Instead, they reveal the cost of treating heterogeneous biodiversity photographs as if each species were represented by one error-free point. For visible reproductive traits, public images provide repeated observations that conventional databases rarely contain at comparable geographic extent. Their value lies in preserving the distribution and its measurement conditions, not in pretending that uncontrolled images are standardized specimens.

The multidimensional species-level PCA reinforces this argument. No single axis captured the orientation, colour and outline endpoints, and the first three components were needed to explain about 69% of species-level variation. A one-dimensional floral syndrome or a single conserved-to-labile ranking would therefore erase both within-species distributions and multivariate trait structure. Comparative datasets should retain at least distributional summaries, sample sizes and assessability information alongside species means.

### Variation and environmental responsiveness are distinct dimensions

The central conceptual result is the separation of visible variation from environmental responsiveness. Across the 102 complete taxa, the two indices were moderately negatively associated, and species occupied all four high-low combinations. High dispersion was therefore neither necessary nor sufficient for strong climate tracking. This distinction matters because both quantities are often described informally as lability.

A species can be highly variable for several reasons without showing a consistent slope along the measured environmental gradients. Variation may be structured by unmeasured local factors, population history, developmental stage, herbivory, phenology or image conditions. Conversely, a species with a comparatively narrow total distribution can show a modest but consistent shift along temperature or precipitation gradients. These situations imply different ecological processes and different predictions under environmental change. A broad phenotype is not automatically responsive, and a responsive phenotype need not be broadly dispersed.

The shape module provides the clearest empirical example. Shape showed the greatest median visible variation but not the greatest climate responsiveness, retained high environmental-niche overlap for most endpoints and lacked globally BH-supported grouped spatial effects. Some of this dispersion probably reflects two-dimensional viewing geometry and segmentation, but that is itself informative for image-derived trait ecology: a visually variable endpoint is not necessarily an environmentally organized endpoint. More generally, studies that report a single variability index risk merging the amount of observed dispersion with the direction and repeatability of environmental association.

### Trait architecture is modular: orientation and colour retain specific environmental structure

Although the overarching result is conceptual, the trait modules retained distinct biological patterns. Orientation produced the largest environmental-niche centroid contrast between species at opposite trait extremes, and climate-only grouped spatial models were preferred. Floral orientation changes the physical and biological interface of a capitulum. Experiments in other systems show that orientation can modify radiative warming, floral temperature and visitor activity (Kevan 1975; Kudo 1995; Patino and Jeffree 2002; Atamian et al. 2016). Orientation also changes exposure to precipitation and the visual and mechanical presentation of a flower head. These pathways make the observed environmental sorting plausible, but our global associations cannot distinguish thermal regulation, rain exposure, pollinator presentation or stem mechanics. In particular, the results do not demonstrate that nodding evolved as a rain-protection adaptation.

Colour occupied a different region of lability space: it had the lowest median total variation but the highest median responsiveness. Hue and chroma also showed relatively strong environmental-niche contrasts, and adding soil improved the preferred grouped models for lightness and chroma. Several non-exclusive mechanisms could generate this pattern. Anthocyanin production and regulation can respond to temperature, radiation, oxidative stress and resource conditions, while evolutionary colour transitions repeatedly involve structural or regulatory changes in pigment pathways (Rausher 2008). At the same time, colour is a receiver-dependent signal. Hymenopteran and other insect visual systems differ from human vision, and ultraviolet reflectance can make human-similar flowers perceptually distinct to visitors (Chittka 1992; Chittka et al. 1994; Briscoe and Chittka 2001).

The image-derived colour endpoints cannot identify pigment molecules, anthocyanin concentration, ultraviolet pattern, pollinator preference or fitness. Soil associations likewise do not establish a direct chemical effect on corolla colour. The appropriate inference is that visible colour showed modest and orderly environmental structure consistent with several physiological and signalling pathways. Resolving those pathways requires calibrated reflectance spectra, floral pigment chemistry, tissue elemental analyses and visitor-specific visual models. The white-pink-purple variation of island *Cirsium* systems provides a promising mechanistic extension because colour origin and contemporary maintenance can be tested separately, but it is not evidence for the mechanism of the global pattern reported here.

### Environmental structure is scale dependent

The apparent strength of trait-environment relationships depended on biological scale and cohort. No primary endpoint-climate combination survived BH correction in the strict <=10 km within-species cohort, whereas an expanded pooled table contained four supported effects of small magnitude. At the same time, species at opposite orientation and colour extremes occupied detectably different environmental space. These findings are not contradictory. They address different questions.

Within-species slopes ask whether observations of the same named species shift consistently along environmental gradients after species-level differences are removed. Among-species sorting asks whether species with different mean phenotypes occupy different environments. Stronger structure at the latter scale can arise through species turnover, longer-term population or lineage differentiation, geographic isolation, unmeasured covariates or unequal environmental coverage within species. Measurement error also attenuates within-species slopes more strongly because the expected signal is smaller. The present results therefore support weak, cohort-dependent contemporary climate tracking embedded within clearer species-level environmental sorting for some modules.

This scale dependence changes how macroecological trait correlations should be interpreted. A species-level association cannot be assumed to represent plastic or population-level response, and a weak within-species slope does not imply that environments are irrelevant to longer-term sorting. Forecasting trait change from species means alone may therefore mistake replacement among species for change within species. Conversely, extrapolating a within-species slope across lineages may ignore historical and taxonomic differences. Explicitly retaining both scales exposes these distinctions.

### Reticulate and polyploid history complicates trait conservatism

The historical analyses did not support a precise, robust species-level explanation for the observed trait architecture. Only 54 of 216 taxa were directly represented in the dated backbone, no non-circular endpoint retained FDR support in the direct-tip subset, and apparent signals for circularity and solidity depended on grafted placements. This result should not be interpreted as evidence that phylogeny is irrelevant. It shows that the available tree and taxon sampling are insufficient for a confident claim of conserved or rapidly evolving capitulum traits.

That limitation is biologically meaningful in the *Carduus-Cirsium* group. Generic and sectional boundaries do not map neatly onto a single molecular history (Ackerfield et al. 2020), cytotype and genome-size diversity occur across Carduinae (Bures et al. 2023), and species-level studies document both ploidy-associated lineage separation and contemporary hybridization (Bures et al. 2018; Michalkova et al. 2023). These processes provide plausible routes to discordance among morphology, taxonomy, plastid history and nuclear history. However, Chapter 1 does not test whether hybridization or polyploidy caused weak signal or transferred particular trait combinations.

The molecular-data audit further showed why opportunistically concatenating available sequences would not solve this problem. ITS, plastid, complete-plastome and sequence-read records were unevenly distributed, and plastid data alone cannot resolve introgression or allopolyploid parentage. A stronger historical test will require deliberately sampled nuclear target-capture or genomic data, explicit cytotype information and network-aware analyses. Until then, tree-placement sensitivity is a more honest result than a single apparently precise phylogenetic coefficient.

### Public-image phenomics complements conventional trait databases

This study also demonstrates what public-image phenomics can contribute when measurement failure is treated as data. The workflow did not simply attach a predicted trait to a species name. It retained provenance-bearing observations, detected repeated reproductive units, used tight and contextual crops for different measurements, extracted continuous endpoints, applied trait-specific assessability rules, tested horizontal-mirror stability and preserved failed measurements. This chain makes the resulting phenomic dataset auditable.

The approach complements rather than replaces TRY, BIEN and standardized field measurements. Public images trade experimental standardization for geographic and within-species replication. That trade can be productive when it is explicit. Replication makes it possible to estimate species distributions, identify taxa in different regions of lability space and target species for standardized follow-up. Assessability models and failure records reveal where the image archive is least informative. Conventional databases remain essential for calibrated traits, controlled protocols and mechanistic interpretation.

The approach also has limits that should shape future public-image studies. iNaturalist observations are not probability samples; observer behaviour and access structure taxonomic and geographic coverage. Detector false negatives can remove observation units before trait quality control begins. Camera processing and lighting alter visible colour. Image vertical is only a proxy for gravity. Two-dimensional outlines depend on viewpoint. Taxonomic errors or broad species concepts can inflate apparent within-species variation. These limitations do not negate the dataset, but they require that uncertainty and sampling structure remain visible in both the analysis and the claims.

### Limitations and next tests

Several submission-critical audits remain to be finalized. Independent detector precision and recall are required because trait-level QC cannot evaluate undetected heads. Measurement demonstration panels must show low, median and high endpoint values, failure categories and original-versus-mirrored measurements. The accepted-name table must be frozen, and residual spatial and broad-region diagnostics must determine whether any headline ranking depends disproportionately on geographic coverage. These audits may strengthen caveats or presentation but should not introduce new headline analyses unless they reveal a material problem in the frozen cohort or measurement system.

Mechanistic follow-up should proceed at smaller spatial and taxonomic scales. Standardized photography and reflectance spectroscopy can separate camera artefacts from true colour differences. Common-garden and reciprocal-transplant experiments can distinguish environmental response from genetic differentiation. Manipulation of head angle combined with floral-temperature logging, simulated rainfall, visitor observations and seed-set measurements can test the functions of orientation. Pigment chemistry and floral transcriptomics can distinguish colour loss, retention and re-expression. Nuclear target capture, flow cytometry and explicit hybrid-network analyses can test whether trait combinations are associated with reticulation or ploidy.

These extensions are particularly important because the global analysis identifies patterns rather than mechanisms. Its role is to show which distinctions matter, which modules retain environmental structure and which taxa occupy unusual combinations of variation and responsiveness. It thereby provides a defensible basis for targeted experiments without converting observational correlations into adaptive explanations.

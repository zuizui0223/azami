# Discussion

## Species means conceal a hierarchy of visible image variation

A species mean is an incomplete representation of visible capitulum phenotype, but the revised decomposition also shows why a single “within-species” fraction is too coarse. Across the nine endpoints, 0.589–0.931 of total visible image sums of squares occurred below the assigned-species mean. Most of this component occurred among photographs within species, while variation among multiple heads in the same photograph contributed 0.143–0.379. Public-image phenotype is therefore hierarchically structured: repeated heads from one image are not independent, yet differences among photographs remain the larger source of below-species variation for every endpoint.

The result persisted after selecting one head per photograph and after repeatedly balancing species to 10 photographs each. These sensitivities weaken the earlier 0.817–0.988 headline range but strengthen the defensible conclusion: for all measured endpoints, at least about half of the visible image variation remained below the assigned-species mean under strong controls for duplicate heads and unequal species replication.

None of these components is equivalent to genetic or phenotypic variance measured under standardized conditions. Among-photograph differences combine populations, individuals, developmental stage, illumination, camera processing, viewpoint and segmentation. Among-head variation in one photograph can include genuine head differences as well as detector-box and occlusion effects. The contribution of public-image phenomics is to expose this hierarchy rather than to label all lower-level variance biological.

The multidimensional PCA strengthens the same argument. No single axis captured orientation, colour and outline, and three components were required to explain about 69% of species-level variation. Comparative datasets based only on means therefore lose both distributional hierarchy and multivariate structure.

## Precision-aware reanalysis removes the apparent negative lability axis

The most important statistical revision concerns the relationship between visible variation and within-species environmental association. The original score averaged absolute, unpooled species slopes. That construction is biased because imprecise slope estimates have large absolute values even when their true effects are zero. The near-perfect correlations of the legacy score with sample size and standard error showed that the previously reported negative relation and median-split quadrants were not biologically interpretable.

After equal module weighting and explicit subtraction or modelling of slope sampling variance, no common relationship remained. The transparent noise-adjusted score was independent of slope sample size and gave a near-zero correlation with visible variation. A hierarchical variance meta-regression using all 2,828 slope estimates and their standard errors reached the same conclusion. We therefore do not infer that highly variable species are systematically less environmentally associated. Instead, the revised result is the absence of a detectable common cross-module coupling.

This correction sharpens the conceptual contribution. Dispersion and spatial environment–trait association remain different quantities, but their distinction is not demonstrated by a negative species ranking. It is demonstrated by their different definitions, hierarchical observation structure, uncertainty and scale-specific evidence. Future cross-species lability indices should model both image hierarchy and slope precision rather than rank raw absolute slopes.

## Trait architecture remains modular

Removing the legacy lability axis does not erase module-specific descriptive patterns. Shape retains the greatest below-species image variance and the largest within-photograph component, consistent with two-dimensional outline measurements being especially sensitive to viewpoint, occlusion and segmentation. Orientation and visible colour show stronger among-species environmental separation than most outline traits. This is not evidence that one module is more adaptively labile than another.

Orientation changes the angle at which capitula encounter radiation, precipitation and visitors. Experiments in other plant systems establish plausible thermal and behavioural pathways, but the global observations cannot distinguish thermal regulation, rain exposure, visitor presentation or stem mechanics. Visible colour likewise reflects camera-recorded lightness, chroma and hue rather than pigment concentration, ultraviolet reflectance or receiver-specific colour space. Mechanistic interpretation requires standardized photography, spectroscopy, pigment chemistry and experiments.

## Environmental structure is scale and cohort dependent

The exhaustive 46,276-observation cohort produced four small BH-supported non-circular linear associations, whereas the earlier balanced-atlas ≤10 km sensitivity produced none. This is not a strict-versus-expanded contradiction; it is a difference between a large post-detection spatially thinned cohort and a smaller balanced atlas. The revised cohort ledger makes this distinction explicit.

Within-species coefficients ask whether observations carrying the same species name shift along spatial climate gradients after species means are removed. Among-species environmental sorting asks whether species with different mean phenotypes occupy different environments. Neither implies temporal response, reaction norms or local adaptation. We therefore replace *climate tracking* and *environmental responsiveness* with *within-species spatial environment–trait association*.

## Public-image phenomics is valuable only when observation error remains visible

Public images provide geographic and within-species replication unavailable in most trait databases, but they trade standardization for scale. Detector false negatives, camera colour processing, image roll, occlusion and broad species concepts can all structure apparent variation. The real-image YOLO and measurement figure demonstrates the production chain, not measurement accuracy. Independent detector and continuous-trait validation remain necessary before final submission.

The revised analyses illustrate a broader methodological lesson: repeated public observations do not automatically solve uncertainty. Multiple detections from one photograph can exaggerate apparent replication, unequal species sample sizes can dominate cross-species slope rankings, and two-level variance partitions can hide the observation hierarchy. Nested decomposition, one-head-per-photo sensitivity, balanced species subsampling, cohort ledgers and precision-aware hierarchical models should be treated as core components of image-based macroecology.

## Historical and taxonomic limits

Only 54 of 216 image-analysis taxa were directly represented in the dated backbone, and no non-circular endpoint retained direct-tip FDR support. Tree placement therefore remains a sensitivity analysis rather than a resolved explanation. An authority-backed taxonomic freeze is equally important because lumped taxa, synonyms and uncertain identifications can alter both within- and among-species variance.

## Remaining tests

Before journal submission, the independent detector audit, continuous-measurement validation, accepted-name table and residual spatial and leave-one-region-out diagnostics must be completed. These tests may change the magnitude of visible variance or individual environmental coefficients. The principal revised claim is deliberately conditional: current data show substantial below-species image dispersion, multidimensional trait structure and module-dependent environmental sorting, but no common coupling between visible variation and within-species climate-association magnitude after slope uncertainty is modelled.

# Chapter 1 final story and analysis boundary

## Central result

Public biodiversity photographs recover several distinct layers of floral-trait
variation that are usually collapsed in species descriptions. Across *Cirsium*,
continuous capitulum traits show climatic structure at the species-comparison
scale, but species differ strongly in how much visible variation they contain and
how closely that variation tracks climate within their ranges.

The final analysis therefore separates two quantities that must not be conflated:

- **within-species phenotypic variation**: how heterogeneous the image-derived
  phenotype is within a species;
- **environmental responsiveness**: how strongly species-specific standardized
  trait values covary with the four predeclared CHELSA predictors.

A species may be highly variable without tracking the measured climate, or may
show a comparatively orderly climate-associated gradient despite modest total
variation. The four-quadrant plot visualizes this distinction.

## Novelty

The strongest novelty is not another global trait–climate regression. It is the
combination of:

1. continuous flower-head traits extracted from public photographs at broad
   taxonomic and geographic scale;
2. explicit modelling of whether each trait is assessable from an uncontrolled
   photograph;
3. separation of among-species climatic structure from within-species climate
   association;
4. decomposition of within-species lability into independent variation and
   response axes;
5. circular treatment of hue rather than treating colour angle as an ordinary
   linear variable;
6. cautious historical sensitivity rather than presenting a grafted mega-tree as
   a resolved evolutionary history.

This creates a reusable framework for turning opportunistic photographs into
comparative phenotypic data without pretending that every visible association is
adaptation or plasticity.

## Recommended headline

**Global floral-trait structure can coexist with weak and heterogeneous
within-species climate tracking.**

The most interesting biological contrast is expected among trait modules:
colour, orientation and outline need not occupy the same position on the
variation–responsiveness plane. Module differences should be described as
contrasting climate association and visible lability, not as evolutionary rates.

## Interpretation limits

- Species-specific slopes are observational associations.
- High responsiveness is not proof of phenotypic plasticity.
- High variation is not proof of polymorphism or genetic differentiation.
- Public-image colour is not spectrally calibrated.
- Image vertical is a proxy for gravity.
- Outline traits remain viewpoint dependent.
- Circular hue joint effects are reported as vector magnitude and direction; a
  scalar-angle linear model is not used.

## Main and supplementary outputs

Main-text candidate:

- species-level four-quadrant plot, if sensitivity analyses show stable rankings;
- compact module comparison of colour, orientation and shape.

Supplement:

- Table S1: all global within-species coefficients;
- Table S2: all species-specific coefficients;
- Figure S1: signed standardized global effects, with hue shown as a joint vector
  magnitude;
- completeness and minimum-sample-size sensitivity tables;
- species and module lability tables.

## Claims to avoid

Do not claim that the analysis demonstrates local adaptation, contemporary
plasticity, pollinator selection, protection from rainfall, evolutionary rate or
causal environmental response. Those require field, common-garden, genomic or
experimental data.

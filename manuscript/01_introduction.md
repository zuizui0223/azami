# Introduction

Comparative trait ecology has transformed the study of biodiversity by representing organisms through measurable attributes that connect performance, environmental filtering and ecosystem function. Global syntheses commonly assign each species one or a few representative trait values, an approach that makes broad comparative analyses possible but also compresses the distribution of phenotypes observed within species. This compression matters because individuals of the same species can differ substantially across populations, environments, developmental stages and local ecological contexts. Intraspecific trait variation can alter species interactions, community assembly and responses to environmental change, and can be large enough to modify conclusions based on species means alone (Bolnick et al. 2011; Albert et al. 2011; Violle et al. 2012; Siefert et al. 2015). Yet global comparative studies still rarely distinguish whether a species is phenotypically variable, whether its phenotype changes consistently along environmental gradients, or whether species with contrasting mean phenotypes occupy different environments. These are related but non-equivalent properties.

Large trait databases such as TRY and BIEN have greatly expanded the taxonomic and geographic scope of plant functional ecology by integrating measurements collected by many independent studies (Kattge et al. 2011, 2020; Maitner et al. 2018). Their principal strength is standardization across heterogeneous sources. Their structure, however, also reflects the constraints of conventional trait collection: measurements are frequently sparse within species, unevenly distributed across geography and environmental space, and often summarized to a species-level value for downstream analyses. Even where multiple records are retained, those observations were rarely designed to recover complete within-species distributions or species-specific environmental slopes at continental to global scales. Consequently, species means remain indispensable for many applications, but they cannot by themselves distinguish broad phenotypic dispersion from repeatable environmental tracking. Nor can they reveal whether relationships observed among species are also expressed within species.

Public biodiversity platforms provide a complementary source of replicated observations. Millions of georeferenced photographs contributed to iNaturalist, GBIF-linked repositories and related citizen-science initiatives record individual organisms across broad spatial, climatic and seasonal gradients. Computer-vision research has shown that such images can support automated species identification, organ detection and phenological classification (Wäldchen and Mäder 2018; Van Horn et al. 2018; Rzanny et al. 2019; Katal et al. 2022). However, most biodiversity-image pipelines have been designed to identify taxa or phenological states rather than to measure continuous traits while retaining observation-level distributions. Studies that infer traits from images also face non-random viewpoint, illumination, occlusion, image quality and photographer-selection effects. A photograph in which a trait cannot be measured is not evidence that the trait is absent. Treating failed measurement as biological absence, or averaging all retained measurements without modelling assessability, can therefore introduce systematic bias.

A further conceptual gap concerns the meaning of trait lability. A species may show a broad observed phenotype without changing predictably along climate gradients, whereas another may occupy a narrow phenotypic range but exhibit a consistent environmental slope. At a larger scale, species with contrasting mean phenotypes may sort among environmental niches even when contemporary within-species associations are weak. Total variation, within-species environmental responsiveness and among-species environmental sorting therefore represent different levels of organization. Collapsing them into a single conserved-to-labile continuum obscures whether variation reflects structured environmental tracking, unmeasured ecological processes, population differentiation, developmental variation or observation error. Resolving these components requires many repeated observations per species, explicit treatment of measurement quality, circular methods for directional traits, spatially structured environmental analyses and sensitivity to uncertain evolutionary history.

Thistles of the Carduus–Cirsium group provide a useful system in which to test this framework. Their capitula vary conspicuously in orientation, corolla colour and external form, allowing several trait modules to be measured on the same reproductive structure. These modules also have different plausible ecological interfaces. Capitulum orientation may alter exposure to precipitation, radiation, convective heat exchange and floral presentation to visitors; corolla colour reflects pigment production and optical signalling and may covary with temperature, water availability, edaphic conditions or pollinator assemblages; and outline traits integrate developmental architecture, structural support and viewing geometry. The group is also evolutionarily and taxonomically complex. Molecular studies have questioned traditional generic and sectional boundaries, while regional studies document hybridization, cytotype differences, polyploid taxa and geographically restricted morphological lineages (Ackerfield et al. 2020; Bureš et al. 2023; Moreyra et al. 2023). These findings do not by themselves demonstrate adaptive radiation, and the present study does not test diversification rates. They do show that a single species mean mapped onto a single fully resolved bifurcating tree is an especially incomplete representation of phenotypic diversity in this group.

Here, we developed a public-image phenomics framework to quantify continuous capitulum traits from globally distributed biodiversity photographs. Rather than assigning images to categorical species-level trait states, we first detected visible capitula, retained both tight and contextual image regions, and extracted continuous measures of orientation, colour and outline. Trait-specific quality control separated assessable from unassessable images, circular hue was represented jointly by sine and cosine components, and technical stability and provenance were retained throughout the pipeline. We then analysed biological scale explicitly. We quantified the fraction of visible variation occurring within and among species, estimated species-specific associations with four predeclared CHELSA climate gradients, compared environmental niches of species with contrasting mean phenotypes, and evaluated whether conclusions were robust to sampling thresholds, spatial structure and uncertain phylogenetic placement.

We addressed four questions. First, how much of the visible variation in capitulum orientation, colour and shape occurs within rather than among species? Second, are species with greater within-species variation also those whose traits more strongly track climate? Third, do orientation, colour and shape differ in their within-species environmental responsiveness and among-species environmental sorting? Fourth, do apparent historical patterns remain stable when analyses are restricted to taxa directly represented in the dated phylogenetic backbone and when alternative placements are propagated? By separating measurement assessability, phenotypic dispersion, within-species climate association, among-species environmental sorting and historical sensitivity, this study tests what is lost when species are represented only by mean trait values and evaluates how public photographs can complement conventional trait databases.

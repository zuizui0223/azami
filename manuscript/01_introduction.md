# Introduction

Comparative trait ecology has transformed the study of biodiversity by linking measurable organismal attributes to performance, environmental filtering, community assembly and ecosystem functioning. Its global reach, however, has depended heavily on representing each species by one or a few characteristic values. That simplification is often necessary, but it also treats phenotypically heterogeneous populations as if they were homogeneous ecological units. Individual and population-level variation can alter interaction strengths, niche breadth, coexistence and responses to environmental change, and its importance depends on both trait and spatial scale (Bolnick et al. 2011; Albert et al. 2011; Violle et al. 2012; Messier et al. 2010). A global synthesis of plant communities further showed that intraspecific trait variation is often substantial relative to interspecific variation, particularly at local scales, although its contribution varies markedly among traits and systems (Siefert et al. 2015). Species means are therefore useful summaries, but they cannot by themselves reveal the distribution of phenotypes within species or distinguish among different sources of that distribution.

This distinction matters because several processes that are commonly described as trait variability or lability are not equivalent. A species may exhibit broad phenotypic dispersion across individuals or populations without changing predictably along an environmental gradient. Conversely, a species may occupy a comparatively narrow phenotypic range while showing a consistent shift with temperature or precipitation. At a broader scale, species with contrasting mean phenotypes may occupy different environmental niches even when contemporary within-species associations are weak. Phenotypic dispersion, within-species environmental tracking and among-species environmental sorting thus represent different biological levels and need not have the same causes. Collapsing them into a single conserved-to-variable axis can confound plasticity, population differentiation, developmental variation, unmeasured ecological structure and observation error. A scale-explicit analysis must retain repeated observations and ask separately where variation occurs and whether that variation is environmentally structured.

Global plant-trait databases have made such questions conceivable across thousands of species. TRY integrates measurements from many studies and has greatly expanded the taxonomic, geographic and functional scope of plant ecology (Kattge et al. 2011, 2020), while BIEN links taxonomic, distributional and trait information through an accessible analytical infrastructure (Maitner et al. 2018). These resources were designed to harmonize heterogeneous data and are indispensable for global synthesis. Their records, however, inherit the sampling structure of conventional trait collection: replication is uneven among species, populations and environments, and downstream analyses commonly aggregate available observations to a species-level estimate. Even when multiple records and coordinates are retained, they rarely provide dense, geographically replicated measurements for the same visible reproductive traits across hundreds of species. Conventional databases and field measurements therefore remain the standard for calibrated functional traits, but a complementary data source is needed to recover observation-level distributions and species-specific environmental relationships at global extent.

Public biodiversity photographs offer that complementary source. Citizen-science platforms contain large numbers of georeferenced observations collected across climatic, seasonal and geographic gradients, and they have become important inputs to biodiversity monitoring and automated identification. Computer-vision studies have demonstrated taxonomic classification and object detection under the highly imbalanced, visually heterogeneous conditions of iNaturalist data (Van Horn et al. 2018), while plant-focused work has shown that organ choice, viewpoint and image context strongly influence identification performance (Wäldchen and Mäder 2018; Rzanny et al. 2019). Image-based phenological classification has also expanded rapidly (Katal et al. 2022). More recent multimodal approaches have begun to infer continuous plant traits from citizen-science images, indicating that these photographs may complement sparse field trait maps rather than serving only as identification records (Sharma et al. 2025). Yet most biodiversity-image pipelines still target species identity or categorical phenological states, and few preserve repeated continuous trait measurements as within-species distributions linked to local environments.

The opportunity is inseparable from substantial bias. Citizen-science observations are uneven across taxa, regions, roads, protected areas and observer communities, and the resulting data reflect accessibility and participant behaviour as well as organism occurrence (Troudet et al. 2017; Di Cecco et al. 2021). Photographs add further non-random variation in illumination, camera processing, distance, occlusion and viewing angle. These effects are especially consequential for colour, orientation and two-dimensional outline. A photograph in which a trait cannot be evaluated is not evidence that the trait is biologically absent, and retaining only the most easily measured images can itself generate environmental or taxonomic bias. Image phenomics therefore requires more than a prediction model: it requires an explicit observation unit, trait-specific assessability, quality-control rules, uncertainty and failure records, and a separation between technical variability and biological interpretation. These requirements have rarely been combined with analyses that distinguish within-species environmental slopes from among-species trait–environment associations.

Thistles in the Carduus–Cirsium group provide a particularly informative system for addressing this gap. Their capitula vary conspicuously in orientation, corolla colour and external form, allowing multiple trait modules to be quantified on the same reproductive structure. These modules plausibly interface with different ecological processes. Floral orientation can alter radiative heating, floral temperature and pollinator visitation, as demonstrated experimentally in heliotropic and directionally fixed flowers (Kevan 1975; Kudo 1995; Patiño et al. 2002; Atamian et al. 2016), while also changing exposure to rainfall and the mechanical presentation of reproductive structures. Corolla colour is shaped by pigment biosynthesis and regulation, but also functions as a receiver-dependent signal: transitions among floral colours can involve changes in anthocyanin pathways (Rausher 2008), whereas ultraviolet reflectance and insect colour vision mean that human-visible hue is only a partial representation of the signal available to pollinators (Chittka 1992; Chittka et al. 1994; Briscoe and Chittka 2001). External form integrates developmental architecture and support, but measurements from uncontrolled photographs also retain viewpoint and opening-stage effects. These distinct ecological and measurement properties make orientation, colour and form useful modules for testing whether trait variation is organized similarly across biological scales.

The group is also evolutionarily and taxonomically complex. Molecular analyses have challenged traditional generic delimitations within the Carduus–Cirsium group (Ackerfield et al. 2020), and phylogenetic work on African mountain thistles has shown that morphologically assigned Cirsium-like lineages can represent distinct evolutionary clades (Moreyra et al. 2023). Cytogenomic studies document genome-size diversity, dysploidy and polyploidy across Carduinae (Bureš et al. 2023). At finer scales, chromosome number, morphology and genetic evidence have separated diploid and tetraploid lineages formerly treated as one species (Bureš et al. 2018), while contemporary hybridization has been documented as a threat to geographically restricted endemic thistles (Michálková et al. 2023). These findings do not establish that Cirsium as a whole is a rapid adaptive radiation, nor do they identify the cause of any trait pattern in the present study. They do make the group a stringent test of approaches that reduce a species to one mean phenotype and assume a single fully resolved bifurcating history.

Here, we developed a public-image phenomics framework to quantify continuous capitulum traits from globally distributed biodiversity photographs. Rather than assigning each species a literature-derived categorical state, we detected individual visible capitula and retained both tight crops and wider contextual regions. We extracted continuous measures of capitulum orientation, corolla lightness, chroma and circular hue, and several outline descriptors. Trait-specific quality control distinguished assessable from unassessable observations; circular hue was analysed through joint sine and cosine components; and technical stability, spatial provenance and failed measurements were retained rather than discarded as preprocessing debris. We then separated biological scales by partitioning visible variation within and among species, estimating species-specific associations with four predeclared CHELSA climate gradients, comparing the environmental niches of species with contrasting mean phenotypes, and evaluating sensitivity to sample size, spatial structure and uncertain phylogenetic placement.

We addressed four questions. First, how much visible variation in capitulum orientation, colour and shape occurs within rather than among species? Second, are species with broader observed phenotypic distributions also those whose traits more strongly track climate within species? Third, do orientation, colour and shape differ in their within-species environmental responsiveness and in the environmental sorting of species with contrasting mean phenotypes? Fourth, do apparent historical patterns remain stable when analyses are restricted to taxa directly represented in a dated phylogenetic backbone and when alternative placements are propagated? By integrating measurement assessability, observation-level trait distributions, within-species climate associations, among-species environmental sorting and historical uncertainty, this study tests what is lost when species are represented only by mean trait values and evaluates how citizen-science imagery can extend, rather than replace, conventional plant-trait databases.

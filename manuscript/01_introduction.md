# Introduction

Comparative trait ecology has transformed the study of biodiversity by linking measurable organismal attributes to performance, environmental filtering, community assembly and ecosystem functioning. Its global reach, however, has depended heavily on representing each species by one or a few characteristic values. That simplification is often necessary, but it also treats phenotypically heterogeneous populations as homogeneous ecological units. Individual- and population-level variation can alter interaction strengths, niche breadth and coexistence, and its importance depends on trait and spatial scale (Bolnick et al. 2011; Albert et al. 2011; Violle et al. 2012; Messier et al. 2010). A global synthesis of plant communities further showed that intraspecific trait variation can be substantial relative to interspecific variation, although its contribution varies markedly among traits and systems (Siefert et al. 2015). Species means are therefore useful summaries, but they cannot reveal the full distribution of phenotypes within species or distinguish among different sources of that distribution.

This distinction matters because several quantities commonly described as trait variability or lability are not equivalent. A named species may contain broad dispersion among observed phenotypes without a consistent spatial association with measured environmental gradients. At a broader scale, species with contrasting mean phenotypes may occupy different environmental space even when within-species associations are weak. Visible dispersion, within-species spatial environment–trait association and among-species environmental sorting represent different statistical and biological levels. Collapsing them into one conserved-to-labile score can confound biological heterogeneity, population structure, developmental variation, unmeasured ecology, observation error and unequal estimation precision. A scale-explicit analysis must retain repeated observations and model uncertainty rather than ranking raw slope magnitudes.

Global plant-trait databases have made such questions conceivable across thousands of species. TRY integrates measurements from many studies and has greatly expanded the taxonomic, geographic and functional scope of plant ecology (Kattge et al. 2011, 2020), while BIEN links taxonomic, distributional and trait information through an accessible analytical infrastructure (Maitner et al. 2018). These resources are indispensable for global synthesis, but replication remains uneven among species, populations and environments, and downstream analyses commonly aggregate observations to a species-level estimate. Even when multiple records and coordinates are retained, dense geographically replicated measurements of the same visible reproductive traits remain uncommon. Conventional databases and field measurements therefore remain the standard for calibrated functional traits, but complementary sources are needed to recover observation-level distributions at global extent.

Public biodiversity photographs offer such a source. Citizen-science platforms contain large numbers of georeferenced observations across seasonal, climatic and geographic gradients and have become important inputs to biodiversity monitoring and automated identification. Computer-vision studies have demonstrated taxonomic classification and object detection under the imbalanced and heterogeneous conditions of iNaturalist data (Van Horn et al. 2018), while plant-focused work shows that organ choice, viewpoint and context influence performance (Wäldchen and Mäder 2018; Rzanny et al. 2019). Image-based phenological classification has expanded rapidly (Katal et al. 2022), and multimodal approaches have begun to infer continuous plant traits from citizen-science images (Sharma et al. 2025). Yet most biodiversity-image pipelines still target species identity or categorical states, and few preserve repeated continuous measurements as within-species distributions linked to local environmental metadata.

The opportunity is inseparable from bias. Citizen-science observations are uneven across taxa, regions, roads, protected areas and observer communities, reflecting accessibility and participant behaviour as well as organism occurrence (Troudet et al. 2017; Di Cecco et al. 2021). Photographs add non-random variation in illumination, camera processing, distance, occlusion and viewing angle. These effects are especially consequential for colour, orientation and two-dimensional outline. A photograph in which a trait cannot be evaluated is not evidence that the trait is absent, and retaining only easily measured images can itself generate environmental or taxonomic bias. Image phenomics therefore requires an explicit observation unit, trait-specific assessability, quality control, uncertainty and failure records. It also requires partial pooling or other precision-aware methods when species differ greatly in sample size.

Thistles in the Carduus–Cirsium group provide an informative system for addressing this gap. Their capitula vary conspicuously in orientation, corolla colour and external form, allowing multiple trait modules to be quantified on the same reproductive structure. These modules have distinct ecological interfaces and measurement constraints. Studies in other flowering systems show that orientation can alter solar loading, floral temperature and pollinator visitation (Kevan 1975; Kudo 1995; Patiño et al. 2002; Atamian et al. 2016). Orientation also changes geometric exposure to precipitation and visitors, although direct evidence that nodding capitula are rainfall adaptations remains limited. Corolla colour is produced by pigment biosynthesis and regulation but functions as a receiver-dependent signal: evolutionary transitions can involve anthocyanin-pathway changes (Rausher 2008), whereas ultraviolet reflectance and insect visual systems mean that human-visible hue captures only part of the signal available to pollinators (Chittka 1992; Chittka et al. 1994; Briscoe and Chittka 2001). External form integrates development and support, but two-dimensional measurements from uncontrolled photographs also retain viewpoint, opening-stage and occlusion effects.

The group is also evolutionarily and taxonomically complex. Molecular analyses have challenged traditional generic delimitations within the Carduus–Cirsium group (Ackerfield et al. 2020), and African mountain thistles assigned to broad traditional genera include distinct evolutionary clades (Moreyra et al. 2023). Cytogenomic studies document genome-size diversity, dysploidy and polyploidy across Carduinae (Bureš et al. 2023). At finer scales, chromosome number, morphology and genetic evidence have separated lineages formerly treated as one species (Bureš et al. 2018), while contemporary hybridization occurs in geographically restricted thistles (Michálková et al. 2023). These findings do not establish that *Cirsium* is a rapid adaptive radiation or explain any pattern in the present study. They make the group a stringent test of analyses that reduce each species to one mean and assume one resolved bifurcating history.

Here, we developed a public-image phenomics framework for globally distributed thistle observations. We detected visible capitula, retained tight and contextual crops and extracted continuous orientation, visible corolla colour and outline measurements. Trait-specific quality control distinguished assessable from unassessable records; circular hue was represented jointly; and spatial provenance and failed measurements remained explicit. We separated a balanced image-comparison atlas from an exhaustive all-photo inference stream, partitioned visible image variance, summarized species-level multivariate structure, estimated within-species spatial associations with four predeclared CHELSA gradients and compared environmental niches among species with contrasting mean phenotypes. We then audited whether cross-species comparisons of slope magnitude were distorted by unequal sample size and replaced the legacy raw absolute-slope score with an equal-module, uncertainty-aware analysis.

We addressed four questions. First, how much visible image variance in capitulum orientation, colour and shape occurs within assigned species rather than among species means? Second, after slope uncertainty is modelled, is visible dispersion associated with the latent magnitude of within-species spatial environment–trait relationships? Third, do orientation, colour and shape differ in visible dispersion and among-species environmental sorting? Fourth, do apparent historical patterns survive restriction to taxa directly represented in a dated backbone and alternative placements? By integrating observation-level distributions, cohort identity, estimation precision, environmental scale and historical uncertainty, this study evaluates what species means hide and what public-image phenomics can add to conventional trait databases.

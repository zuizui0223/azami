# Chapter 1 novelty review

Review date: 2026-07-11  
Status: provisional literature audit for manuscript positioning, not a claim of
exhaustive priority.

## Scope of the search

The review asked whether an existing study already combines the following six
elements:

1. global or multi-continental *Cirsium* sampling;
2. continuous capitulum orientation, colour and outline measurements;
3. automated measurement from public/citizen-science photographs;
4. explicit modelling of image assessability or QC retention;
5. separation of within-species and between-species climate associations;
6. sensitivity to uncertain species placement across alternative historical
   trees.

Searches combined terms including:

- `Cirsium capitulum orientation climate phylogeny`
- `Cirsium flower colour climate comparative`
- `Cardueae floral traits phylogenetic comparative`
- `Cirsium morphology macroecology`
- `citizen science photographs plant traits macroecology`
- `image-derived floral traits climate`
- `iNaturalist flower colour traits ecology`
- `automated plant trait extraction public photographs`

The search covered general web and scholarly-indexed results accessible through
web search. Before submission, the same concepts should be searched in Web of
Science, Scopus and Google Scholar, with citation chaining from the nearest
papers.

## Provisional conclusion

No directly matching study was located in the initial search.

The defensible novelty claim is therefore not that any individual method is new.
The likely novelty is the **combined study design and biological application**:

> a global, continuous, image-derived analysis of multiple capitulum traits in
> *Cirsium* that models differential assessability, contrasts within- and
> between-species climate structure, and propagates uncertainty in broad-scale
> historical placement.

This should be written as `to our knowledge` until the structured database search
is completed.

## What is not novel

### Automated use of biodiversity photographs

Computer vision has been widely applied to plant identification and is now being
extended to trait inference. Recent work such as PlantTraitNet demonstrates that
citizen-science photographs can support global inference of plant height, leaf
area, specific leaf area and leaf nitrogen when uncertainty and external
validation are addressed.

Therefore, Chapter 1 must not claim to be the first use of citizen-science images
for plant traits or global trait mapping.

### Image-based flower detection and phenotyping

Automated flower detection, segmentation and image-based phenotyping are well
established in agriculture and controlled phenotyping. The distinction here is
not the existence of image processing, but its use on uncontrolled public
photographs for comparative capitulum ecology.

### Ecological functions of floral orientation

Floral orientation has known biological functions in other systems. Mature
sunflower orientation influences warming and pollinator visitation, and flower
structures or downward orientation can protect water-susceptible pollen from
rain. Chapter 1 therefore must not present orientation as a newly recognized
functional floral trait.

The study also does not experimentally identify which function applies to
*Cirsium*.

### Floral colour and outline as ecological traits

Colour, shape and display architecture are standard topics in pollination
biology and floral evolution. The novelty is not that these traits may matter,
but that they are measured continuously at broad scale in a difficult thistle
genus and analysed with an explicit measurement-bias layer.

### PGLS, Pagel lambda and alternative trees

Phylogenetic generalized least squares, Brownian covariance and Pagel lambda are
standard comparative methods. Multiple-tree sensitivity is also established
practice when tree uncertainty is available.

In this project, the historical analysis is additionally limited by the fact
that most taxa are grafted into a vascular-plant backbone rather than represented
by a resolved *Cirsium* species tree.

## Nearest literature and the remaining gap

## 1. *Cirsium* and Cardueae systematics

The closest genus-level literature focuses on delimitation, phylogeny,
reticulation, polyploidy and biogeography. Ackerfield and colleagues showed that
the traditional Carduus–Cirsium group has serious generic-delimitation problems.
Hyb-Seq work in Cardueae provides a broader nuclear/plastid framework and temporal
context.

These studies establish why a single plastid or synthetic tree should not be
presented as a fully resolved species history. They do not appear to provide a
global continuous analysis of head orientation, colour and outline against
climate.

**Gap retained by Chapter 1:** broad phenotypic macroecology rather than a new
systematic reconstruction.

## 2. Functional studies of floral orientation

Experimental and species-specific studies show that orientation can affect
thermal environment, pollinator visits and protection from rain. Examples
include east-facing mature sunflower heads and rain-protective floral structures
in other angiosperms.

**Gap retained by Chapter 1:** cross-species global variation in thistle head
orientation, while avoiding a direct mechanistic claim.

## 3. Citizen-science imagery and plant traits

The literature increasingly supports automated trait inference from public or
large image collections. Most work emphasizes species identification,
agricultural flower detection, standardized specimen imagery, or vegetative
traits such as height and leaf economics.

**Gap retained by Chapter 1:** uncontrolled close-up images used to derive
multiple continuous reproductive/head traits, followed by an ecological analysis
of the measurement-retained and measurement-failed samples.

## 4. Trait–environment comparative analyses

Comparative studies relating floral traits to pollination, climate or geography
are common across angiosperms. However, the initial search did not locate a study
that performs the present combination within *Cirsium* while separately fitting
within-species and between-species climate associations.

**Gap retained by Chapter 1:** the explicit scale contrast. The result that
between-species structure is present while strict within-species effects are not
is part of the contribution, not an inconvenient null result.

## Strongest novelty components

Ranked from strongest to weakest:

1. **Scale separation:** testing the same trait–climate question within and among
   species rather than treating thousands of observations as independent species
   replicates.
2. **Measurement-selection audit:** modelling whether colour, orientation and
   outline are measurable as a function of taxon, region and climate.
3. **Multi-trait continuous capitulum atlas:** colour, directed orientation and
   outline extracted on one reproducible scale across 216 analysis taxa.
4. **Historical uncertainty rather than one authoritative tree:** conclusions
   tested across alternative grafting placements, with direct and grafted tips
   explicitly distinguished.
5. **Public-image reproducibility:** failed measurements, mirror replicates,
   aggregation rules and provenance retained rather than hidden.

## Novelty that should not be claimed

Avoid the following statements:

- `the first automated plant-trait study using citizen science images`;
- `the first global plant-trait map from photographs`;
- `the first demonstration that flower orientation is adaptive`;
- `the first phylogenetic analysis of Cirsium`;
- `the first evidence that climate drives Cirsium head traits`;
- `a resolved global Cirsium phylogeny`;
- `proof that downward heads evolved as rain protection`;
- `proof of pollinator-mediated selection`.

## Recommended novelty wording

### Conservative abstract wording

> We developed a reproducible workflow to quantify continuous capitulum colour,
> outline and image-referenced orientation from public photographs across 216
> *Cirsium* taxa. Unlike conventional species-level trait compilations, the
> analysis explicitly modelled trait assessability, separated within-species from
> between-species climate associations, and evaluated the latter across
> alternative historical placements.

### Introduction wording

> Public biodiversity images are increasingly used for taxonomic recognition and
> plant-trait inference, but their use in comparative floral macroecology remains
> constrained by uncontrolled viewpoint, lighting and non-random trait
> assessability. To our knowledge, these sources have not been used to jointly
> quantify multiple continuous capitulum traits across *Cirsium* while separating
> within- and between-species climatic structure and propagating broad-scale tree
> uncertainty.

### Discussion wording

> The contribution lies less in any individual image-processing or comparative
> method than in their integration: continuous reproductive-trait measurement,
> explicit modelling of assessability, scale-separated ecological inference and
> sensitivity to uncertain historical placement.

## Current novelty verdict

**Promising but conditional.**

The paper appears to occupy a real gap, especially in the combination of
continuous capitulum measurement, explicit QC-selection analysis and
within-versus-between species inference. The safest claim is:

> To our knowledge, this is the first global comparative analysis in *Cirsium*
> to derive multiple continuous capitulum traits from public photographs while
> explicitly modelling assessability and testing scale-dependent climatic
> structure under alternative historical placements.

Retain `to our knowledge` until the structured database audit is complete.
